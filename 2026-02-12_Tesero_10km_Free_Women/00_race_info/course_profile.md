# Course Profile: Women's 10km Interval Start Free

## Venue

**Cross Country and Biathlon Center Fabio Canal** (formerly Lago di Tesero Cross-Country Stadium)
- **Location:** Village of Lago, Municipality of Tesero, Val di Fiemme, Trentino, Italy
- **Stadium Elevation:** ~830 m above sea level ([NBC Olympics](https://www.nbcolympics.com/news/cross-country-skiing-101-venue))
- **Coordinates:** 46.28 N, 11.51 E (approximate)
- **Total Trail Network:** 19 km of FIS-homologated trails ([Olympics.com](https://www.olympics.com/en/milano-cortina-2026/venues/tesero-cross-country-skiing-stadium))
- **Spectator Capacity:** 15,000 ([NBC Olympics](https://www.nbcolympics.com/news/cross-country-skiing-101-venue))

## Course Configuration

The Women's 10km Interval Start Free uses **three laps** on two distinct loop configurations:

| Lap | Loop Color | Nominal Distance | Actual Length | Height Difference (HD) | Max Climb (MC) | Total Climb (TC) |
|-----|-----------|-----------------|---------------|----------------------|----------------|------------------|
| 1 | Red 3.75 km | 3.75 km | 3,549 m | 59 m | 46 m | 129 m |
| 2 | Blue 3.75 km | 3.75 km | 3,783 m | 63 m | 31 m | 156 m |
| 3 | Blue 2.5 km | 2.5 km | 2,629 m | 38 m | 29 m | 105 m |

### Course Totals

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Total Actual Length** | 9,961 m (~10 km) |
| **Total Climb** | 390 m (129 + 156 + 105) |
| **Maximum Height Difference** | 63 m (Blue 3.75 km loop) |
| **Maximum Single Climb** | 46 m (Red 3.75 km loop) |

### Key Course Metrics Explained

- **Height Difference (HD):** The difference in elevation between the highest and lowest points on the loop.
- **Max Climb (MC):** The longest single uninterrupted ascent on the loop.
- **Total Climb (TC):** The cumulative vertical meters of ascending across all uphill sections on the loop.

## Loop Analysis

### Lap 1: Red 3.75 km Loop (3,549 m actual)

The Red loop features the **steepest single climb** of the entire course at 46 m, making it the most demanding individual ascent athletes will face. Despite having the lowest total climb of the three laps (129 m), the concentrated nature of the climbing -- with a 46 m maximum climb within a 59 m height differential -- indicates a course with a sharp, decisive uphill section followed by a corresponding technical descent. This loop is shorter than its nominal distance suggests (3,549 m vs. 3,750 m), indicating a tighter, more technical layout.

**Tactical implications:** The steep 46 m climb early in the race will test athletes' power output while they are still fresh. Strong V1/herringbone climbers may gain an early advantage. The lower total climb (129 m) relative to the other laps suggests more rolling terrain between the major climb and descent.

### Lap 2: Blue 3.75 km Loop (3,783 m actual)

The Blue 3.75 km loop is the **most demanding lap by total climb** (156 m) and has the greatest height difference (63 m). However, the maximum single climb is only 31 m, indicating that the climbing is distributed across multiple shorter ascents rather than one sustained hill. This creates a relentlessly undulating profile that rewards consistent pacing and efficient transitions between uphill and downhill technique.

**Tactical implications:** The 156 m of total climbing across relatively short ascents demands excellent metabolic management. Athletes who can maintain high speeds through rolling terrain without large power surges will perform well. The slightly longer actual distance (3,783 m) suggests wider, more sweeping turns compared to the Red loop.

### Lap 3: Blue 2.5 km Loop (2,629 m actual)

The final lap is shorter (2,629 m actual) with 105 m of total climbing and a 38 m height difference. The maximum climb of 29 m indicates moderate, distributed climbing. This is the finishing loop where the race will be decided.

**Tactical implications:** As the final lap, athletes will be operating at significant fatigue levels. The 105 m of total climb on a shorter loop means the climbing-per-kilometer ratio remains high (~40 m/km, compared to ~36 m/km on Lap 1 and ~41 m/km on Lap 2). Athletes with superior endurance and the ability to maintain technique under fatigue will gain their final advantages here.

## Overall Course Character

With 390 m of total climbing across just under 10 km, this course averages approximately **39.2 m of climbing per kilometer** -- a demanding course by World Cup standards. For reference:

- The course rewards **climbers and all-around athletes** over pure flat-speed specialists.
- The mix of one steep climb (Red loop, 46 m MC) and distributed climbing (Blue loops, 29--31 m MC) means athletes need both explosive climbing power and sustained climbing endurance.
- The **freestyle (skating) technique** allows athletes to use V2, V2 alternate, and V1 techniques depending on gradient, favoring those with strong double-poling capacity and efficient gear changes.

## Stadium and Finish Area

The start/finish stadium has been extensively renovated for the 2026 Olympics. The grandstand in the finish area was demolished and rebuilt, with new lodging facilities and offices for timekeepers and judges. New underground spaces house changing rooms, technical rooms for ski technicians, testing areas for athletes, and an equipment preparation center. The pistes were widened to a minimum of 9 metres, and a climb was built after the start area. Walkways and tunnels for spectators were also constructed. ([Nordic Mag](https://www.nordicmag.info/en/2026-olympics-tesero-cross-country-skiing-stadium-everything-you-need-to-know-about-the-val-di-fiemme-stadium-which-will-host-the-cross-country-skiing-events-at-the-milan-cortina-2026-olympics-0103/), [Fiemme World Cup](https://www.fiemmeworldcup.com/en/Winter-Olympic-Games-2026/Cross-country-stadium))

The renovation cost for the 2026 Olympics was estimated at EUR 22.4 million. Snowmaking systems, lighting, and venue technology were modernized and improved. ([NBC Olympics](https://www.nbcolympics.com/news/cross-country-skiing-101-venue))

This is one of the most storied cross-country skiing venues in the world. Athletes competing in the 10 km will be highly familiar with this terrain:

### Tour de Ski Connection

The iconic Tour de Ski Final Climb at Alpe Cermis in nearby Cavalese concludes every edition of the Tour de Ski. The final stage is 9.0 km with the climb itself being 3.6 km at an average gradient of 11.6% and a maximum gradient of 28.0% -- nicknamed "Monster Bakken" by the Norwegians. While the Olympic 10 km course does not use the Alpe Cermis climb, the flat and rolling portions of the Tour de Ski stages use the same Lago di Tesero trail network that forms the basis of the Olympic courses. ([Fiemme World Cup -- Tour de Ski](https://www.fiemmeworldcup.com/en/tour-de-ski/history-and-curiosities), [Wikipedia -- Tour de Ski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tour_de_Ski))

## Snowmaking Infrastructure

The course benefits from a comprehensive artificial snowmaking system that draws water from the adjacent Avisio river. This system was introduced during the 2003 World Championships renovations and has been repeatedly upgraded -- most recently for the 2026 Olympics. The artificial snow ensures reliable coverage from December to mid-March regardless of natural snowfall, though artificial snow crystals tend to be harder and more abrasive than natural snow, which has implications for wax selection and ski base preparation. ([Visit Fiemme](https://www.visitfiemme.it/en/activities/skiing/cross-country-skiing), [NBC Olympics](https://www.nbcolympics.com/news/cross-country-skiing-101-venue))
