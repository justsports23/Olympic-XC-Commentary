## Course Description

The sprint course at the Cross Country and Biathlon Center Fabio Canal in Tesero is a purpose-built loop of 1,585 meters — significantly longer than typical World Cup sprint circuits of 1,200–1,400 meters. It features **49 meters of total climbing**, with a **largest single climb of 23 meters** and two sharp hairpin corners added during the Olympic redesign.

The course's defining feature is a **long, sustained climb near the end of the lap** that "exposes anyone who arrives underpowered or out of position" (FasterSkier). At the Tour de Ski test event on January 3, 2026, Klæbo opened a three-second gap on this climb alone. Gus Schumacher described the course as "pretty long… it favors a guy like me—capacity." Julia Kern described it as having "technical downhills, big climbs—it has everything."

**This is a climber's sprint course, not a bunch-sprint finish.** The length and climbing profile strongly favor athletes with sustained power output over pure flat-track speed.

### Key Characteristics
- **Classic technique only** — no skating/freestyle; athletes must use diagonal stride, double-pole, and herringbone techniques
- **Significantly longer than typical WC sprint courses** — 1,585m vs. 1,200–1,400m norm
- **49m of climbing** — this is a substantial vertical gain for a sprint, making it one of the most demanding sprint courses on the circuit
- **Two sharp hairpin corners** — added during Olympic redesign, creating tactical choke points where positioning is critical
- **Stadium finish** — flat finishing straight through the purpose-built stadium with grandstands

### Tactical Sections
1. **Start Zone / Stadium Exit** — critical for establishing position in knockout rounds; the first hairpin corner creates an early choke point
2. **First Climb** — moderate climb that starts to separate the field
3. **Technical Descent** — downhill sections where ski speed (glide wax) and confidence at speed matter; positioning through hairpin corners is race-decisive
4. **Second / Main Climb (23m)** — the defining feature; a long, sustained climb near the end of the lap where the race is won and lost. Klæbo opened a 3-second gap here in January
5. **Descent to Stadium** — recovery zone for those who survived the climb; ski speed on descents proved decisive at the January test event
6. **Stadium Re-entry / Finish Straight** — flat finishing stretch where remaining sprint speed decides places

## Altitude Considerations
- Racing at **~840m elevation** — altitude effects are **negligible**
- This is significantly lower than Davos (~1,560m) or Livigno (~1,816m)
- Altitude is not a differentiating factor at this venue
- However, the elevation creates **snow-quality variability** as temperatures swing from well below freezing at qualification time to near zero by the afternoon final

## Classic Technique Implications
The classic technique requirement is a critical filter for this race:
- **Favors:** Athletes with strong diagonal stride and herringbone technique (Klæbo, Valnes, Chappaz, Grahn)
- **Disadvantages:** Pure freestyle sprint specialists (Chanavat, Grond, Vuorinen) who are less proven in classic
- **Double-poling on flats** is important but the climbing sections demand full classic technique
- The long course length rewards endurance-type sprinters over pure speed specialists

## Historical Course Context
The Val di Fiemme venue has hosted sprints at the Tour de Ski (annually), the 1991, 2003, and 2013 FIS Nordic World Ski Championships, and numerous World Cup events. The terrain profile strongly favors well-rounded sprinters with powerful classic technique and climbing ability. The January 3, 2026 Tour de Ski sprint on this exact course serves as the single most predictive dataset for the Olympic race — 84 athletes from 23 nations competed that day.
