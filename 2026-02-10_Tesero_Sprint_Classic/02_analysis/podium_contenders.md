# Podium Contenders — Men's Sprint Classic
## Milano Cortina 2026 Winter Olympics | February 10, 2026

---

## Gold: Johannes Høsflot Klæbo (NOR)

**Probability: Overwhelming (~90%+ barring fall/DQ/equipment failure)**

The probability of anyone else winning approaches zero in the absence of a fall, equipment failure, or disqualification. The evidence:

1. **Won on this exact course** 5 weeks ago (Tour de Ski, Jan 3) — fastest qualifier (3:17.64), won final by +3.11 seconds
2. **Won all 4 classic sprints this season:** Ruka, Trondheim (100th WC win), Val di Fiemme, Goms
3. **Already won skiathlon gold** at these Games (Feb 8) — form and confidence are peak
4. **Leads both overall and sprint World Cup standings**
5. His classic sprint technique is the best in the world — no one else combines his speed, power, climbing ability, and tactical awareness

The only precedent for a Klæbo sprint loss at a championship was his DQ at the 2021 Worlds 50km — not a sprint event. His sprint heats record in championships is virtually unblemished.

**Expected approach:** Push the pace on the second climb in every heat, daring others to follow. He opened a 3-second gap on this climb in January.

## Silver/Bronze Contenders (ranked by likelihood)

### 1. Erik Valnes (NOR) — Silver favorite if healthy

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Classic sprint form | Won Ruka WC sprint (Nov); 2nd only to Klæbo |
| Championship pedigree | Olympic team sprint gold (2022); multiple WC medals |
| Heat-racing ability | Elite — thrives in tactical chaos |
| Course fit | Strong climber; classic technique is world-class |
| **Risk** | **Battling a cold — illness could compromise performance on demanding 1,585m course** |

If his cold clears, Valnes's WC sprint win this season and championship pedigree make him the strongest bet for silver. His classic sprint is elite, and his experience alongside Klæbo in training gives him course and race-intelligence advantages.

### 2. Jules Chappaz (FRA) — Highest upside, highest risk

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Course-specific result | **2nd on this exact course** in January (+3.11s behind Klæbo) |
| Season form | Breakthrough classic sprint season |
| **Risk** | **Fell seriously ill in late January; arrived Feb 7; first training Feb 9** |
| Olympic experience | **Debut** — first-ever Olympic race |

Chappaz produced the performance of the season when he finished 2nd on this course in January. If truly recovered from his illness, his course knowledge and proven ability on this terrain make him a genuine medal contender. If not recovered, he may not make the final.

### 3. Federico Pellegrino (ITA) — Championship magic on home soil

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Championship pedigree | 2x Olympic sprint silver, 2025 Worlds sprint silver |
| Tactical brilliance | Unmatched positioning through tight corners |
| Home crowd | Vocal Italian support; farewell race narrative |
| **Risk** | **Classic sprint form inconsistent this season (DNQ at Ruka, 25th qual at Val di Fiemme)** |

Never count out a four-time Olympian in a farewell race on home snow. His tactical brilliance and ability to elevate in championship finals are legendary. However, this course's long climb may expose age-related power limitations (he is 35) against younger legs. A top-6 final appearance is likely; a medal requires his best-ever classic sprint performance.

### 4. Anton Grahn (SWE) — Course-proven dark horse

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Course-specific result | **3rd on this exact course** in January |
| Age/pressure | 21, Olympic debut — big stage could overwhelm or inspire |
| Title | U23 World Champion in classic sprint |

His podium on this exact course in January is the strongest course-specific evidence any dark horse could present. If the moment doesn't overwhelm the 21-year-old, he is a legitimate medal threat.

### 5. Edvin Anger (SWE) — Redemption-seeking powerhouse

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| FIS sprint ranking | 4th in the world (9.39 points) |
| Season form | 3rd at Goms classic sprint (Jan) |
| Physique | 1.90m, powerful double-poling; endurance profile matches this course |
| **Risk** | **Skiathlon disaster (fell, broke poles, 37th) — emotional recovery needed** |

His classic double-poling power and endurance profile match this course well. The skiathlon disaster makes this sprint a crucial redemption opportunity — but the emotional residue of that failure could cut either way.

| Medal | Athlete | Nation | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
